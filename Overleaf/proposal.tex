% ── 1. Research Positioning ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Research Positioning}

\textbf{Supervisor's framework}: How to build a system that translates grid carbon intensity signals into real-time data center scheduling decisions, using Virtual Capacity Curves (VCC) derived from ElectricityMaps data and Google Cluster Traces \citep{colangelo2025}.

\textbf{This thesis}: Within that framework, how can a \emph{deterministic mathematical programming model}, rather than heuristic rules, minimize AI data center carbon emissions through joint temporal and spatial workload scheduling across five grid regions?

The supervisor defines the overall pipeline (VCC generation, workload classification, simulation architecture). This thesis formalizes the Carbon-Aware Optimization component into a provably optimal LP, deepening and extending the existing framework.

  How can a deterministic mathematical programming model, using carbon intensity (CI) and carbon-free energy fraction (CFE) as scheduling signals, minimize AI data center carbon emissions through joint temporal and spatial workload scheduling across five grid regions?
\end{quote}

% ── 4. Indicators ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Core Indicators}

\paragraph{Carbon Intensity (CI).}
The primary optimization signal, measured in gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh and fully comparable across regions. Retrieved directly from the ElectricityMaps \texttt{carbonIntensity} field \citep{radovanovic2023}.

\paragraph{Carbon-Free Energy Fraction (CFE).}
CFE measures the share of electricity consumption met by carbon-free sources: all variable renewables (wind, solar, hydro) \emph{plus} nuclear, computed from the ElectricityMaps \texttt{powerBreakdown} field. CFE is strictly broader than the Renewable Fraction (RF): a grid running on nuclear baseload has RF $\approx 0$ but CFE $\approx 1$, while a gas-heavy grid has both near zero. This distinction matters for scheduling: two hours with identical CI may have very different CFE, revealing whether low emissions stem from a dispatched clean asset (stable, foreseeable) or from wind surplus (variable, curtailment-sensitive). EDA regression of CI on CFE across all five regions (Section~10) determines whether the multiplicative discount $\mathrm{CI}_r(t)\cdot(1-\alpha\cdot\mathrm{CFE}_r(t))$ is appropriate or whether the relationship is better captured by an additive or region-specific form; the final functional form is data-driven.


% ── 5. Methodology ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

The core methodology is deterministic optimization. Historical CI and CFE data are used directly as model inputs, and evaluation is performed via backtesting over a two-year historical window.

{\small\[
  \underbrace{\text{Historical CI/CFE (ElectricityMaps)}}_{\text{Input}}
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \underbrace{\text{Deterministic Optimization (LP via HiGHS)}}_{\text{Core contribution}}
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \underbrace{\text{Backtesting vs.\ baselines}}_{\text{Evaluation}}
\]}

\subsection{Unified Joint Optimization Model}

Rather than separating temporal and spatial shifting into sequential phases, the model optimizes both dimensions simultaneously. The decision variable \(x_{r,t} \geq 0\) denotes the compute load (kW) assigned to region \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) at hour \(t \in \mathcal{T}\), and covers both the \emph{when} and the \emph{where} of scheduling in a single formulation.

\subsubsection{Objective Function}

\begin{equation}
  \min_{x_{r,t},\,M} \left\{
    \underbrace{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}}
      x_{r,t} \cdot \text{CI}_r(t) \cdot \bigl(1 - \alpha \cdot \text{CFE}_r(t)\bigr)}_{\text{(i) carbon cost}}
  \;+\;
    \underbrace{\gamma \sum_{r \neq r_0} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} x_{r,t}}_{\text{(ii) transfer cost}}
  \;+\;
    \underbrace{\eta \cdot M}_{\text{(iii) equity}}
  \right\}
  \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Interpretation.}

\textit{Component (i): Carbon cost.}
The term $x_{r,t} \cdot \text{CI}_r(t) \cdot (1 - \alpha \cdot \text{CFE}_r(t))$ is the carbon attributed to one unit of flexible load. $\text{CI}_r(t)$ is the base carbon cost; the factor $(1 - \alpha \cdot \text{CFE}_r(t))$ then discounts it according to how clean the consumed electricity is.

Consider two hours that both have $\text{CI} = 100$ gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh. In the first, high wind and nuclear output push CFE to 0.8; at $\alpha = 0.5$, the effective cost is $100 \times (1 - 0.5 \times 0.8) = 60$. In the second, demand is low overnight but the generation mix is still fossil-heavy, so CFE is 0.1; the effective cost is $100 \times (1 - 0.5 \times 0.1) = 95$. A pure CI minimiser treats both hours identically. The CFE discount correctly assigns a lower cost to the first, steering the scheduler toward hours where low emissions reflect actual clean generation rather than a temporary drop in demand.

At $\alpha = 0$, the discount disappears entirely and the model reduces to pure CI minimisation. At $\alpha = 1$, a fully carbon-free hour (CFE\,=\,1) carries zero effective cost. EDA OLS regression of CI on CFE across the five regions yields implied values of 0.83--1.00 for PJM, NYISO, Finland, and Belgium, so the CFE signal is empirically strong, not just a theoretical add-on. The baseline uses $\alpha = 0.5$ (conservative); sensitivity analysis covers $\alpha \in \{0,\,0.5,\,0.8,\,1.0\}$. Singapore is a partial exception: CFE $\approx 2.7\%$ there, so the discount contributes negligibly regardless of $\alpha$.

Raw CI values are not normalised. Cross-region comparability requires this: Finland at $\text{CI} \approx 72$ gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh and Singapore at $\text{CI} \approx 567$ gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh carry a roughly eight-to-one carbon-cost ratio. Per-region normalisation would collapse both to the same $[0,1]$ scale, destroying the spatial scheduling signal entirely.

\textit{Component (ii): Transfer cost.}
$\gamma$ is a cost in gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh applied to every unit of flexible load routed outside the home region $r_0$. It represents how much additional carbon the scheduler is willing to incur in order to avoid an off-home transfer. At $\gamma = 0$, there is no such cost and the optimiser routes freely. As $\gamma$ increases, the scheduler gradually prefers local execution even when a remote region has lower CI.

The scale of $\gamma$ must be calibrated against the actual CI gap between regions. In our dataset, median CI for PJM (home) is 466 gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh and for Finland is 72; to make PJM competitive, $\gamma$ would need to exceed roughly 400 gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh, a value with no direct operational counterpart. For this reason, the hard geographic cap in C4 is a more practical instrument when strict locality is required, and $\gamma$ is varied across a range in sensitivity analysis to trace the trade-off between carbon reduction and data-transfer cost.

For all three objective terms to be dimensionally consistent in gCO\textsubscript{2}eq, $\gamma$ must carry units of gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh. This keeps the weighted sum \eqref{eq:objective} interpretable: each unit represents a gram of CO\textsubscript{2}-equivalent.

\textit{Component (iii): Equity.}
$\eta$ is a scalar weight on the auxiliary variable $M$. $M$ represents the carbon footprint of the single most-burdened region: constraint C5 forces $M$ to be at least as large as the flexible-load carbon of every region, so it takes the value of the regional maximum. For example, if PJM accumulates 10{,}000 gCO\textsubscript{2} of flexible-load carbon and Finland accumulates 1{,}000 gCO\textsubscript{2}, then $M = 10{,}000$. Minimising $\eta \cdot M$ penalises schedules that concentrate carbon in one region.

At $\eta = 0$, the equity term is absent and the model pursues total carbon minimisation only. At $\eta = 0.3$, the solver accepts an increase of 0.3 gCO\textsubscript{2} in total carbon in order to reduce the worst-case regional carbon by 1 gCO\textsubscript{2}. In the two-year backtest, $\eta = 0.3$ increased total carbon by 1{,}450 gCO\textsubscript{2} (+2.8\%), indicating that geographic equity can be achieved at modest aggregate cost in this dataset \citep{li2024equitable}.

The three parameters $\alpha$, $\gamma$, $\eta$ each control one dimension of the objective. Setting any to zero removes that dimension and recovers a simpler baseline, which makes the contribution of each term independently verifiable.

This thesis uses CFE as a real-time dispatch signal from the ElectricityMaps API, which differs from \citet{riepin2024spatiotemporal}, who treat 24/7 CFE matching as an investment-planning constraint under dedicated renewable asset ownership. Those authors decide which assets to build; this thesis decides when and where to consume grid electricity given existing infrastructure.

\subsubsection{Decision Variable}

The model optimises over $x_{r,t} \geq 0$: the flexible load assigned to region $r$ at hour $t$, in kWh. Flexible workloads are those that can be deferred or relocated without violating job deadlines, such as batch training runs, offline inference, and data preprocessing jobs.

The model's scope is deferrable workloads. Real-time inference, heartbeat jobs, and other latency-critical tasks run unconditionally and are handled at the system layer outside the LP. The LP optimises only the flexible portion. A noted limitation is that task-level trace data are not available for the five study regions, so the share of total load that is deferrable cannot be quantified empirically; this is left for future work.

When $\eta > 0$, a scalar auxiliary variable $M$ is introduced: it is constrained to be at least as large as the carbon total of each region, so it takes the value of the regional maximum. This linearises the $\max_r(\cdot)$ term so the equity objective remains a linear program. When $\rho < 1$, per-region auxiliaries $U_r \geq \max_t x_{r,t}$ and $L_r \leq \min_t x_{r,t}$ are introduced to linearise the peak-to-trough difference in C7.

\subsubsection{Constraints}

\begin{align}
  &\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} x_{r,t} = D^{\text{flex}}
    \tag{C1}\label{eq:c1} \\[4pt]
  &\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{t'=\tau}^{\min(\tau+\delta,\,|\mathcal{T}|)} x_{r,t'} \geq D_{\tau}^{\text{flex}} \quad \forall\,\tau \in \mathcal{T}
    \tag{C2}\label{eq:c2} \\[4pt]
  &C_{\min,r} \;\leq\; x_{r,t} \;\leq\; C_{\max,r} \quad \forall\, r,\, t
    \tag{C3}\label{eq:c3} \\[4pt]
  &\sum_{r \neq r_0} x_{r,t} \;\leq\; \sigma \cdot \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} x_{r,t} \quad \forall\, t
    \tag{C4}\label{eq:c4} \\[4pt]
  &M \;\geq\; \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} x_{r,t} \cdot \text{CI}_r(t) \quad \forall\, r \in \mathcal{R}
    \tag{C5}\label{eq:c5} \\[4pt]
  &\bigl|x_{r,t} - x_{r,t-1}\bigr| \;\leq\; \kappa \cdot C_{\max,r} \quad \forall\, r \in \mathcal{R},\; t \geq 1
    \tag{C6}\label{eq:c6} \\[4pt]
  &\max_{t \in \mathcal{T}}\, x_{r,t} \;-\; \min_{t \in \mathcal{T}}\, x_{r,t} \;\leq\; \rho \cdot C_{\max,r} \quad \forall\, r \in \mathcal{R}
    \tag{C7}\label{eq:c7}
\end{align}

\textbf{C1:Total demand completion.}
All flexible demand must be served over the planning horizon. This is the only constraint that forces the model to schedule anything at all; an LP with no demand-completion requirement would just set every $x_{r,t}$ to zero and call that optimal.

\textbf{C2:Latency bound.}
Each batch of flexible demand $D_{\tau}^{\text{flex}}$ arriving at hour $\tau$ must be fully served within $\delta$ hours, so the optimiser cannot push everything out to a single clean hour far in the future at the cost of every job's deadline. The effect is measurable at both scales tested. In the perturbation test (Table~\ref{tab:perturbation}), tightening $\delta$ from 6\,h to 1\,h removes access to FI's lowest-CI hours and raises carbon from 2{,}574 to 2{,}808\,gCO\textsubscript{2}eq. Across the two-year backtest, tightening $\delta$ from 48\,h to 6\,h raises LP carbon by 8.2\,\% on average (Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}); $\delta = 48$\,h and $\delta = 24$\,h produce identical results, confirming that a 24-hour planning window already captures the full available scheduling opportunity. The baseline sets $\delta = 24$\,h; sensitivity analysis covers $\delta \in \{6, 12, 24, 48\}$\,h.

\textbf{C3:Regional capacity bounds.}
The flexible load at each region-hour is bounded: $C_{\min,r} \leq x_{r,t} \leq C_{\max,r}$. The upper bound reflects physical supply and cooling limits per region; the lower bound prevents a region from going completely idle, which would incur restart costs when load returns. Non-negativity of $x_{r,t}$ follows automatically from $C_{\min,r} \geq 0$, so no separate constraint is needed for it. The integration test in Section~\ref{sec:validation} checks that both bounds hold exactly at every timestep.

\textbf{C4:Geographic transfer limit (optional).}
At each hour, the load scheduled outside the home region $r_0$ cannot exceed fraction $\sigma$ of the total dispatched load: $\sum_{r \neq r_0} x_{r,t} \leq \sigma \cdot \sum_r x_{r,t}$. The bound is relative to dispatched load rather than total demand, so it stays meaningful regardless of how much demand is deferred. Setting $\sigma = 1$ removes the constraint entirely. In testing, tightening $\sigma$ from 1.0 to 0.3 increased carbon by a factor of 5.5, a direct read on the carbon cost of data-locality restrictions. $\sigma$ is treated as a sensitivity parameter.

\textbf{C5:Equity auxiliary (active when $\eta > 0$).}
$M$ must be at least as large as the flexible-load carbon of each region, $M \geq \sum_t x_{r,t} \cdot \text{CI}_r(t)$ for all $r$, so at the optimum it equals the regional maximum. The solver has no incentive to keep $M$ tight on its own: without C5 it would simply set $M = 0$ and the equity term in the objective would do nothing. When $\eta = 0$, the equity term is absent from the objective and C5 is dropped from the model.

\textbf{C6 (Ramp rate, active when $\kappa < 1$).}
The load change between consecutive hours is capped at $\kappa \cdot C_{\max,r}$: a data center cannot instantaneously reassign large volumes of compute due to hardware warm-up, network reconfiguration, and job-queue inertia. The concept follows \citet{wijayawardana2025scheduling}, who characterise this as \emph{step size}. Because the absolute-value form is not LP-compatible, the implementation replaces C6 with two one-sided inequalities ($x_{r,t} - x_{r,t-1} \leq \kappa \cdot C_{\max,r}$ and $x_{r,t-1} - x_{r,t} \leq \kappa \cdot C_{\max,r}$), which together are equivalent. In testing, $\kappa = 0.1$ (limit = 10 kWh/h) forced 66.7 kWh out of the clean window and into higher-carbon hours, raising total carbon from 8{,}000 to 31{,}333 gCO\textsubscript{2}; the constraint is genuinely binding, not just present in the formulation.

\textbf{C7:Dynamic range (active when $\rho < 1$).}
The peak-to-trough load swing in region $r$ over the full horizon cannot exceed $\rho \cdot C_{\max,r}$. A scheduler with no such limit can exploit carbon variability by swinging a region between near-zero and full capacity; this is operationally disruptive to cooling systems and power procurement, even if it minimises carbon on paper. The concept follows \citet{wijayawardana2025scheduling}, who term this \emph{dynamic range}. The LP implementation introduces per-region auxiliary variables $U_r \geq \max_t x_{r,t}$ and $L_r \leq \min_t x_{r,t}$, so C7 becomes three linear constraints: $x_{r,t} \leq U_r$, $L_r \leq x_{r,t}$, and $U_r - L_r \leq \rho \cdot C_{\max,r}$, together enforcing the peak-to-trough limit without nonlinearity. In testing, $\rho = 0.4$ (limit = 40 kWh on a 100 kWh region) held the swing exactly at 40 kWh and raised carbon from 12{,}500 to 21{,}250 gCO\textsubscript{2}.

\subsection{Model Validation}
\label{sec:validation}

The validation strategy follows a single, specific recommendation: take a small instance, keep every constraint active simultaneously, then perturb one input at a time and check that the solution reacts the way it should. This is a behavioural check, not a constraint-satisfaction check: the question is whether the model responds correctly when an input changes, which a one-off solve cannot answer.

\subsubsection{Perturbation Scenario Checks}

The instance uses three real regions, PJM (home, dirty), Finland (FI, cleanest), and Belgium (BE, medium), over a 6-hour window drawn from 2024-07-17 00:00--05:00~UTC. Real CI values are used throughout: PJM at 564--613~gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh, FI at 52--55, BE at 100--138. The correct answer is verifiable before the solver runs, because the CI gap between FI and PJM is large enough that the optimal routing is unambiguous. All seven constraints are active simultaneously ($\sigma=0.6$, $\kappa=0.5$, $\rho=0.7$, $\eta=0.1$). Four inputs are perturbed one at a time from a common baseline; Table~\ref{tab:perturbation} reports the headline metric for each, and Figure~\ref{fig:f1} shows the full before/after picture.

Every metric moves in the predicted direction. The capacity squeeze is the most informative case, because it tests two different predictions rather than one. $C_{\max}$ is a per-hour cap, not a per-window cap, so a mild squeeze (panel C, middle bar) should leave the region's total load completely unchanged: the LP has six hours to spread the same load over, so it never needs to send any of it elsewhere. Only once the squeeze is severe enough that even spreading across the full window cannot fit the preferred load (panel C, right bar) should the total actually drop and the shortfall move to another region. Both predictions hold exactly: 6.0\,kWh unchanged under the mild squeeze, dropping to 3.0\,kWh with the remaining 3.0\,kWh picked up elsewhere under the severe one.

This case also caught a real error during development: an earlier version of the test used only the mild squeeze and concluded, incorrectly, that capacity constraints have no effect in this regime. The conclusion was an artefact of a missing numerical tolerance: a floating-point rounding residual on the order of $10^{-16}$ satisfied a strict greater-than comparison and produced a pass for the wrong reason. Adding a tolerance margin and the severe-squeeze case to actually force overflow resolved this; the result above is the corrected one.

\subsubsection{Solver and Heuristic Fallback}
The model is solved as a Linear Program (LP) over continuous variables using the HiGHS solver via \texttt{scipy.optimize.linprog}. If workload discretization is required, the formulation upgrades to a Mixed Integer Program (MIP); given the large-scale horizon (\(|\mathcal{R}|=5\), \(|\mathcal{T}| = 17{,}544\) hours over two years), LP relaxation is the default.

If the LP solve time proves prohibitive for rolling-window backtesting, a \textbf{greedy heuristic} is implemented as a fallback: at each decision window, sort region-hour pairs by effective cost $\text{CI}_r(t)\cdot(1-\alpha\cdot\text{CFE}_r(t)) + \gamma\cdot\mathbf{1}[r \neq r_0]$ and greedily assign load to the cheapest feasible pairs subject to capacity and transfer constraints. The equity term is omitted from the greedy sort key since it is a global objective that cannot be decomposed per region-hour. The heuristic provides a near-optimal bound and serves as an additional baseline for benchmarking solution quality.

Following the one-at-a-time (OAT) sensitivity analysis convention for LP models with few parameters \citep{saltelli2008}, Figure~\ref{fig:f3} reports the result as a tornado diagram \citep{eschenbach1992}: each parameter is swept across its full tested range while the others are held at their loosest (inactive) values, and the resulting LP carbon is expressed as a percentage change from the fully unconstrained baseline. Bars are sorted by impact width, so the dominant parameter appears at the top.

The dominant finding is that $\sigma$, the geographic cap, determines the effective range of achievable savings far more than any other parameter: tightening $\sigma$ from 1.0 to 0.3 increases LP carbon by 373.7\,\% relative to the unconstrained baseline. By contrast, $\eta$ (+20.2\,\%), $\delta$ (+8.2\,\%), $\rho$ (+2.9\,\%), $\kappa$ (+2.1\,\%), and $\alpha$ (+0.2\,\%) each have an order of magnitude less impact. At the tested magnitudes, geographic data-locality requirements limit achievable carbon savings far more than either temporal smoothing or fairness constraints do.

\subsubsection{Why $\kappa$, $\rho$, and $\alpha$ have a small aggregate effect}

A small aggregate effect is not, by itself, evidence that a parameter is inactive, and this was checked explicitly rather than assumed. For $\kappa$ and $\rho$: because each window injects a single demand batch, the unconstrained LP's natural solution always concentrates that batch into one region-hour slot, so the ramp constraint is at its structural maximum (a 100\,\% ramp) in every one of the 731 windows: $\kappa$ and $\rho$ are binding in 100\,\% of windows, not a minority of them. Their carbon cost is small not because they rarely bind, but because the region the LP prefers usually has a locally smooth CI profile around its optimal hour, so spreading the batch over a few additional hours to satisfy the ramp/range cap costs little carbon. The same mechanism explains why C6 and C7 are clearly binding yet carbon-cheap in the validation instance (Table~\ref{tab:validation}).

For $\alpha$: comparing the optimal allocation at $\alpha=0$ versus $\alpha=1$ directly (not just the resulting carbon) on a sample of 100 windows shows the allocation differs in 43 of them, sometimes substantially. The CFE discount is doing something real to the schedule. The reason the aggregate carbon barely moves is that CI and CFE are strongly negatively correlated within each region (Pearson $r=-0.98$ for Finland and Belgium, $-0.87$ for NYISO, $-0.61$ for PJM): the hours the discount makes more attractive are usually already low-CI hours, so the discount rarely redirects the schedule toward a meaningfully dirtier hour. Singapore is the exception ($r=-0.24$, reflecting its near-flat 2.7\,\% CFE share), where $\alpha$ has essentially nothing to act on regardless of its value.

\subsection{Heuristic Analysis}
\label{sec:heuristic}

Following the convention used to report carbon savings against a carbon-agnostic baseline \citep{acun2023carbonexplorer}, Figure~\ref{fig:f5} places every scheduler on a single axis: the carbon saving each achieves relative to the no-optimisation baseline,
$$\text{saving} = \frac{C_{\text{Uniform}} - C_{\text{alg}}}{C_{\text{Uniform}}} \times 100\,\%,$$
where positive values save carbon relative to doing nothing and negative values do worse. All schedulers are run under the same full operational constraints ($\sigma=0.6$, $\kappa=0.5$, $\rho=0.7$, $\delta=24$\,h). The LP saves $31.4\,\%$ over the no-optimisation baseline; the $78.4\,\%$ headline figure in Section~\ref{sec:decomp} is a different scenario, the unconstrained setting ($\sigma=1.0$).

The comparison spans four schedulers chosen to bracket the LP from above and below. Oracle, the LP solved over a 168-hour look-ahead window, saves $36.8\,\%$. It exceeds the operational LP not because it is a better policy but because it assumes a full week of perfect future CI knowledge, which is unavailable at deployment time; it serves as a ceiling, not a competitor.

Greedy sorts region-hour pairs by effective cost $\text{CI}_r(t)(1-\alpha\cdot\text{CFE}_r(t))$ and fills the cheapest slots first, without solving an LP. It saves $31.5\,\%$, statistically indistinguishable from the LP on this dataset. This closeness is not a general guarantee: Greedy enforces only the geographic cap $\sigma$, silently violating the ramp ($\kappa$) and range ($\rho$) limits that a deployed system must respect. Its near-optimal carbon here is a property of this data, not a property of the algorithm, which is precisely the distinction a provably constrained LP is designed to remove.

FCFS fills the earliest available slots within $\delta$ hours of arrival using the home region first, with no carbon information. It saves $-51.5\,\%$, meaning it emits roughly half as much carbon again as doing nothing, because PJM has consistently high CI and front-loading concentrates demand into its dirtiest hours. This is practically significant because FCFS is a common default in production job schedulers.

Read together, the figure shows that the LP captures essentially all of the carbon saving available to a realistically-informed scheduler ($31.4\,\%$ against Oracle's $36.8\,\%$ ceiling), while being the only method in the comparison that both attains this saving and provably respects every operational constraint. Greedy matches it only by chance and only by ignoring constraints; FCFS, the status-quo default, is actively harmful. The ranking is stable across all four seasons (Figure~\ref{fig:f5}, right).

\subsection{Decomposition and Cross-Region Analysis}
\label{sec:decomp}

\textbf{Temporal versus spatial decomposition.} A key question about the LP's performance is how much of the 78.4\,\% carbon saving comes from routing load to cleaner regions (spatial flexibility) versus scheduling load at cleaner times within a region (temporal flexibility). Following the three-variant decomposition design used to separate these contributions in the carbon-aware scheduling literature \citep{sukprasert2024limitations}, the two-year backtest is re-run under three restricted LP variants:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Temporal only}: $\sigma \approx 0$ forces all load to remain in the home region (PJM); the LP may only shift load across hours within PJM.
  \item \emph{Spatial only}: $\delta = 1$ removes deferral; the LP routes load across regions but cannot delay any demand.
  \item \emph{Joint} (baseline): both dimensions active, $\sigma = 1$, $\delta = 24$.
\end{itemize}

The marginal saving of each dimension is computed relative to the uniform baseline, and an interaction term captures the additional value of combining both. Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:decomp} and Figure~\ref{fig:f6}.

Temporal scheduling within a dirty grid is actively counterproductive when the alternative is to distribute load more broadly.

The interaction term (38.4\,\% overall, 48.9\,\% in summer) is the extra saving from doing both together: routing load to a clean region and then picking its cheapest hours beats either move alone. The interaction peaks in summer, when Finland's diurnal CI swing is largest and there is more for the timing half of the decision to work with.

\textbf{Cross-region variation: CV regression.} To explain why some regions offer more temporal scheduling opportunity than others, the coefficient of variation (CV $= \sigma_\text{CI}/\mu_\text{CI}$) of hourly CI is computed per region over the full two-year dataset and compared against the per-region temporal saving, defined as the saving achieved by a single-region LP relative to uniform allocation within that region alone (Figure~\ref{fig:f7}).

Finland (CV $= 0.42$) and Belgium (CV $= 0.40$) show the largest within-region temporal savings, at 13.3\,\% and 27.7\,\% respectively. PJM, NYISO, and Singapore have low CV (0.11--0.13) and correspondingly low temporal savings (5.2--7.5\,\%). The positive relationship between CV and temporal saving is what we would expect: without CI variability over time, there is nothing for temporal shifting to exploit. Belgium's higher saving relative to Finland despite similar CV reflects a more pronounced diurnal renewable pattern, concentrated in daytime solar hours, which the LP can exploit more systematically than Finland's less predictable wind profile.

% ── 7. Expected Contributions ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Expected Contributions}

\paragraph{C1:Unified joint LP for carbon-aware scheduling.}
Existing approaches either optimise temporal shifting and spatial load balancing sequentially, or rely on rule-based heuristics rather than provably optimal formulations \citep{liu2012, radovanovic2023}. This thesis formalises both dimensions into a single LP solved jointly over a rolling window, without pre-imposing a sequential decomposition.

\paragraph{C2:CFE-weighted objective with empirically determined discount.}
Prior CI-only objectives treat two hours with identical carbon intensity as equivalent, regardless of whether the low emissions arise from clean generation or a transient demand trough \citep{radovanovic2023}. The objective function $\text{CI}_r(t)\cdot(1-\alpha\cdot\text{CFE}_r(t))$ adds a multiplicative CFE discount that rewards structurally clean grids. The weight $\alpha$ is not assumed a priori: EDA regression of CI on CFE across five regions yields implied values of 0.83--1.00, and sensitivity analysis covers $\alpha \in \{0, 0.5, 0.8, 1.0\}$. This distinguishes the approach from \citet{riepin2024spatiotemporal}, who treat 24/7 CFE matching as an investment-planning constraint under dedicated asset ownership; here CFE is used as a real-time dispatch signal from the public ElectricityMaps API, requiring no dedicated procurement.

\paragraph{C3:Empirical evaluation across five structurally distinct grids.}
The model is backtested over two years of hourly data across PJM, NYISO, Finland, Belgium, and Singapore, five regions that span a wide range of generation mix, CI level, and temporal variability. The coefficient of variation (CV) is used as a diagnostic to explain cross-regional differences in scheduling savings, and a post-hoc decomposition isolates the marginal contribution of temporal versus spatial flexibility to total carbon reduction.
